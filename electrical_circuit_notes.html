1. Introduction to Electrical Circuits

Definition:

An electrical circuit is a closed conducting path through which an electric current can flow continuously.

Need & Characteristics:

  • Provides a designated path for energy transfer.
  • Must contain at least one Active Element (Source).
  • Contains Passive Elements (Load) to consume/store energy.
  • If the path breaks, current becomes zero (Open Circuit).

Fundamental Formula:

V = I × R
(Ohm's Law: I = V/R, P = V × I)
📝 Exam Point: An open circuit has infinite resistance (R = ∞) and zero current (I = 0). A short circuit has zero resistance (R = 0) and maximum current.

2. Linear Circuit

Definition:

A circuit whose parameters (Resistance, Inductance, Capacitance) remain constant with respect to changes in time, voltage, or current.

Characteristics:

  • Strictly obeys Ohm's Law (V ∝ I).
  • Satisfies the Superposition Principle.
  • Waveform of input and output remains the same (no distortion).

Examples:

Pure Resistive circuits, purely inductive or capacitive networks, Air-core inductors.

Applications:

Filters, impedance matching networks, and basic power distribution.

I (Current) V (Voltage) 0
Fig 1.2: V-I Graph (Straight Line)
✔ Advantages
  • Mathematical analysis is very easy.
  • Output is highly predictable.
✖ Disadvantages
  • Cannot perform logical operations.
  • Cannot amplify signals.
💡 Memory Tip

Linear = "Line"-ar.
The V-I graph is always a straight line passing through the origin.

3. Non-Linear Circuit

Definition:

A circuit where the parameters change depending upon the applied voltage, current, or time.

Characteristics:

  • Does not strictly obey Ohm's Law.
  • Superposition theorem is invalid.
  • V-I relationship is a curve, not a straight line.

Examples:

Diodes, Transistors (BJT/FET), Iron-core inductors, Transformers.

✖ Disadvantages
  • Analysis is mathematically complex.
  • Generates harmonics (distortion).
